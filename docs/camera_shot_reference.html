Production Reference

Camera Shot
Language

Industry-standard terminology for shot sizes, angles, camera movement, composition, and editing. Use these terms when directing, prompting, or collaborating on edit decisions.

Shot size

Extreme wide shot (EWS)

aka: Extreme long shot (ELS)

Subject tiny in environment. Establishes scale, isolation, location.

Wide shot (WS)

aka: Long shot (LS), full shot

Full body visible with room to breathe. Context + character together.

Medium wide shot (MWS)

aka: Cowboy shot

Thighs to top of head. Named for Western gun-draw framing.

Medium shot (MS)

aka: Mid shot, waist shot

Waist to head. Workhorse shot — neutral, conversational.

Medium close-up (MCU)

aka: Bust shot

Chest to head. Common for interviews, dialogue, emotional presence.

Close-up (CU)

Face fills frame. Emotion, reaction, intensity. The emotional workhorse.

Choker

aka: Tight CU

Chin to forehead only. Tighter than a CU. Claustrophobic intensity.

Extreme close-up (ECU)

aka: XCU, detail shot

Eyes, lips, hands, object detail. Maximum intimacy or tension.

Insert shot

Tight cut to an object or detail (hands on keys, clock face). Punctuates action.

Camera angle

Eye level

Camera at subject's eye height. Neutral, equal, relatable.

Low angle

aka: Hero shot, worm's eye (extreme)

Camera below subject looking up. Power, dominance, menace.

High angle

Camera above looking down. Vulnerability, smallness, surveillance.

Bird's eye

aka: Top-down, overhead, god shot

Straight down 90°. Patterns, choreography, scope. Detached perspective.

Dutch angle

aka: Dutch tilt, canted angle

Camera rolled off axis. Unease, disorientation, psychological tension.

Three-quarter / 45° angle

aka: Three-quarter front

Between front-on and profile. Most flattering for faces. Very common in narrative.

Over the shoulder (OTS)

Shooting past one person's shoulder at another. Dialogue, perspective, relationship.

Point of view (POV)

aka: Subjective shot

Camera is the character's eyes. Total immersion, first-person intensity.

Profile / side angle

aka: 90° shot

Camera at 90° to subject's eyeline. Cinematic, graphic, stylized.

Camera movement

Pan

Camera head rotates left/right on fixed axis. Follows action or reveals space.

Tilt

Camera head rotates up/down on fixed axis. Reveal height, reveal character top-to-toe.

Whip pan

aka: Swish pan

Extremely fast pan that motion-blurs between shots. Energy, humor, scene transition.

Dolly

aka: Track, truck

Camera physically moves toward/away (dolly in/out) or sideways (truck/crab). Smooth cinematic depth.

Dolly zoom

aka: Vertigo shot, Hitchcock zoom, trombone shot

Dolly out + zoom in simultaneously (or reverse). Background warps — dread, revelation.

Crash zoom

aka: Snap zoom

Extremely fast zoom in (or out). Shock, comedy, sudden emphasis. Common in action and horror.

Push in / pull out

Slow dolly move toward or away from subject. Emotional emphasis, intimacy or distance.

Pedestal

aka: Ped up / ped down

Camera moves straight up or down on its axis without tilting. Different from a tilt — the lens stays level.

Crane / jib

aka: Boom shot

Camera moves up or down on an arm. Epic reveals, God-like perspective shifts.

Handheld

aka: Vérité, shaky cam

Organic, slight camera shake. Energy, realism, chaos, urgency.

Steadicam / gimbal

Stabilized follow move. Smooth but fluid — neither locked off nor handheld.

Roll

Camera rotates on the lens axis (barrel roll). Distinct from Dutch tilt — this is a moving rotation, not a static cant.

Orbit / arc

aka: Circular dolly, 360

Camera circles subject. Heroic reveal, disorientation, dramatic emphasis.

Drone / aerial

Unmanned aerial movement. Scale, geography, reveal, transition between scenes.

Composition

Rule of thirds

Subject placed on grid intersections, not centered. Natural visual weight.

Symmetrical framing

aka: Center framing, Kubrick framing

Subject dead center with mirrored environment. Formal, unsettling, highly stylized.

Negative space

Deliberate empty area around subject. Creates tension, isolation, or visual breathing room.

Leading lines

Environmental lines (roads, corridors, fences) that draw the eye toward the subject.

Headroom / lead room

Space above head (headroom) or in direction subject faces/moves (lead room).

Two-shot

Two subjects in frame together. Relationship, tension, dialogue.

Single

One subject isolated in frame. Focus, intimacy, isolation.

Foreground / background

aka: FG / BG

Elements staged in layers for depth. Selective focus separates planes.

Rack focus

Focus shifts from FG to BG (or reverse) mid-shot. Directs attention, reveals story.

Editing

Hard cut

Immediate shot change. The default cut. Most common edit in any timeline.

J-cut / L-cut

Audio leads video (J) or video leads audio (L). Smooths transitions, creates flow.

Match cut

Two shots connected by visual similarity (shape, motion, color). Metaphor, continuity.

Jump cut

Cut within same scene, same angle — intentional jolt. Time compression, stylization.

Smash cut

Abrupt cut at peak tension/emotion to contrasting scene. Humor, shock, contrast.

Cross-cut

aka: Parallel editing

Cuts between two simultaneous events. Builds tension, shows connection.

Cutaway

Cut to related but non-continuous shot (reaction, environment). Context or breathing room.

Cut on action

Cut mid-movement — door opening, hand reaching. Hides the edit in motion.

Dissolve

aka: Cross dissolve

Gradual blend between two shots. Passage of time, softness, memory.

Fade to black / fade in

Shot gradually darkens to black (or rises from it). Finality, scene beginning, passage of time.

Wipe

One shot pushes another off frame in a direction. Stylized, classic (Star Wars).

Montage

Rapid sequence of shots to compress time or build emotional momentum. Training sequences, passage of time.
